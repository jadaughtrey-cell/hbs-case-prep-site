Case: HBS 9-723-051 (32pp) + AI Policy Note 9-723-018 (12pp)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ASSIGNMENT QUESTIONS
Q1: How does the concentration of frontier AI capabilities among a handful of firms alter the competitive dynamics that have historically kept monopoly power in check? Is antitrust policy, as currently structured, a sufficient response?
Economic: AI creates unprecedented scale economies and data network effects — incumbents accumulate training data advantages that new entrants cannot replicate. Unlike Standard Oil (geographic) or AT&T (infrastructure), AI moats compound: more users → more data → better model → more users. Political: Regulatory lag is severe; current antitrust frameworks were designed for physical markets. Remedies like structural separation are harder to apply to models than to pipelines. Strategic: Key leverage point is compute access (H100 clusters), not just data. Export controls on chips inadvertently reinforce the oligopoly by blocking foreign challengers without addressing domestic concentration.
Q2: Jensen Huang has argued generative AI renders traditional software labor increasingly obsolete. Who captures the productivity gains from this shift, and what mechanisms exist to distribute them more broadly?
Labor: Coding automation compresses median programmer wages while amplifying senior engineers — a "superstar" effect that increases within-skill variance. Real wage impact depends on whether new tasks emerge fast enough to offset displacement. Capital vs. Labor: Historical technology transitions (electrification, steam) ultimately raised labor share after a 20–40 year transition. AI may differ in speed and scope. Piketty's r>g dynamic is relevant. Distribution: Profit-sharing mandates, robot taxes, expanded public goods financed by AI gains. Singapore's Skills Future model as counterexample to pure market allocation.
Q3: How should a country with no domestic frontier AI capabilities approach national AI policy? Is AI dependency fundamentally different from dependency on oil or semiconductors?
Dependency: Oil is a depleting commodity; AI is an infinitely reproducible service consumable without owning the means of production. This lowers barriers to use but raises sovereignty concerns — data jurisdiction, model alignment with local values, kill-switch risk. Strategic options: (1) Consumer sovereignty — negotiate data and fine-tuning rights; (2) Open-source stack — build on Llama/Mistral derivatives to reduce US/China dependence; (3) Regulatory arbitrage — attract AI firms with permissive fine-tuning environments. Geopolitics: US/China bifurcation creates a Brussels Effect dynamic — the EU regulatory approach exports globally. Countries must choose a tech stack aligned with their political economy.
