Taiwan and the Global Semiconductor Industry
BGIE · Class 26 · Thursday, April 23, 2026 · 1:00 PM – 2:20 PM
CourseBGIE — Business, Government & the International Economy
CaseTaiwan and the Global Semiconductor Industry (HBS 9-722-032)
Key ActorsTSMC (Morris Chang), U.S. CHIPS Act, Chinese semiconductor policy, Samsung
Scale~$600B global semiconductor market; TSMC controls ~54% of foundry revenue
Core TensionEconomic interdependence vs. national security; globalization vs. strategic autonomy
Executive Summary
  • Taiwan's semiconductor dominance — particularly TSMC's monopoly on leading-edge logic chips — is the product of deliberate industrial policy, geographic concentration of talent, and decades of compounding technical investment, not accident.
  • The U.S., China, and EU are all pursuing domestic semiconductor self-sufficiency simultaneously, but foundry-grade fabs take 5–10 years and $20–40B each to build, making rapid decoupling economically irrational and physically impossible in the short run.
  • Taiwan's semiconductor industry functions simultaneously as a "silicon shield" (deterrence via mutual dependence) and a strategic vulnerability (single point of failure for the global tech economy if conflict disrupts production).
  • The deglobalization risk is real but asymmetric: countries can nationalize the design layer (fabless) far more quickly than the manufacturing layer (fab), meaning partial decoupling is already underway even as full decoupling remains a generation away.
Key Timeline
1973Taiwan government establishes ITRI; begins deliberate semiconductor industrial policy.
1987Morris Chang founds TSMC — first pure-play foundry. Invents the fabless/foundry model.
2015–20China's "Made in China 2025" targets semiconductor self-sufficiency; $150B+ state funding committed.
2020–22Global chip shortage exposes supply chain fragility. U.S. restricts Huawei access to TSMC fabrication.
2022U.S. CHIPS Act passes ($52B); sweeping export controls block advanced chips and equipment to China.
2024–26TSMC Arizona fabs face cost overruns and talent gaps. China continues domestic investment despite lagging 2–3 nodes.
Argument Landscape
PositionSupporting EvidenceCounterargument
Semiconductor industry is source of protection ("silicon shield") Global tech economy depends on uninterrupted TSMC production; conflict would devastate both aggressor and global markets; mutual dependence deters escalation Shield only works if all parties are rational economic actors; military/ideological goals can override economic cost; shield did not deter Russia in Ukraine despite economic interdependence

Q&A — Canvas Questions
Q1. Assess the effects of government policies on the development of the semiconductor industry in the United States, Taiwan, and China.
  • Taiwan: ITRI-led industrial policy in the 1970s–80s seeded talent and IP; government subsidized land, utilities, and R&D; export-oriented strategy forced TSMC to compete globally, driving efficiency. Result: concentrated dominance at leading edge.
  • United States: Cold War-era defense spending built the foundational semiconductor industry (Bell Labs, Intel). Post-1990s, market-driven model allowed manufacturing to offshore while fabless design thrived. CHIPS Act (2022) represents belated industrial policy reversal — $52B in subsidies, but culture, talent, and cost structures still disadvantage U.S. fabs vs. Taiwan.
  • China: $150B+ committed via "Big Fund" and provincial subsidies. Result: significant progress in mature nodes (28nm+) and memory (YMTC), but export controls cut off EUV lithography access, blocking the path to leading-edge logic below 7nm. State subsidies have produced quantity, not quality at the frontier.
  • Takeaway: Industrial policy works when it builds on comparative advantage (Taiwan) or addresses genuine market failures (early U.S. defense R&D). It struggles when it tries to compress decades of learning curves with capital alone (China at leading edge).
Q2. How should Taiwan navigate the rise of Sino-American tensions? Is its semiconductor industry a source of protection or vulnerability?
  • Both simultaneously: The industry is a shield against full-scale invasion (global economic devastation would follow) but a vulnerability to coercion, blockade, or targeted strikes that stop short of invasion.
  • Taiwan's strategic options: (1) Deepen the shield — keep most advanced production on-island to maintain deterrence value. (2) Internationalize selectively — TSMC Arizona/Japan plants signal alliance value without fully surrendering the shield. (3) Accelerate indigenous defense — semiconductor revenues fund military modernization.
  • The key risk: If TSMC disperses production too aggressively, Taiwan loses its unique strategic asset. If it concentrates entirely, it remains a single point of failure that invites coercion. The optimal strategy is calibrated internationalization that preserves leading-edge concentration in Taiwan while building goodwill via overseas investment.
Q3. Does competition over Taiwan and semiconductors augur deglobalization? What consequences might follow?
  • Partial deglobalization is already locked in: Export controls, CHIPS Act, and allied coordination have structurally bifurcated the ecosystem. A fully unified global semiconductor supply chain is no longer politically achievable in the near term.
  • Consequences of deglobalization: (1) Higher chip costs — redundant fabs raise average cost per wafer significantly, passed through to consumer electronics, autos, and industrial goods. (2) Slower innovation — fragmented ecosystems lose cross-pollination effects. (3) Geopolitical alignment pressure — every country's semiconductor strategy signals alliance posture. (4) Emerging market exclusion — countries without fab subsidies risk becoming technology deserts.
  • The floor: Full decoupling is economically irrational; the more likely outcome is partial bifurcation at the leading edge (U.S./allied vs. China) with continued integration at mature nodes and chip design — a world of "managed interdependence" rather than clean decoupling.
Hidden Assumptions & Risks
Key Assumption

The "silicon shield" assumes rational economic deterrence. If China decides Taiwan reunification is worth the economic cost, the deterrence logic collapses — as Russia demonstrated that interdependence doesn't prevent conflict.

Hidden Risk

TSMC's moat is not just equipment — it's 35 years of tacit process knowledge embedded in its workforce. Even a perfectly equipped competitor fab would take a decade to replicate TSMC's yield rates. This is systematically underweighted in policy discussions.

Canvas Scrape
canvas_scraper.py
The sandbox environment that runs the pipeline cannot reach hbs.instructure.com directly — outbound requests are blocked at the proxy layer. The workaround: browser-side JavaScript (fetch_canvas_assignments.js) runs inside the user's active HBS browser session, calls the Canvas REST API at hbs.instructure.com/api/v1/courses/{id}/assignments using the user's stored token, and returns structured JSON. That JSON is written to canvas_assignments_cache.json on disk. canvas_scraper.py detects the proxy block automatically and falls back to the cache — no manual intervention needed. The cache contains per-course assignment metadata, due dates, and the assignment questions extracted from Canvas.
⚠️ Current limitation: This two-step fetch (browser → cache → Python) adds a manual trigger. An enterprise deployment with direct server-to-Canvas API access would eliminate it — Canvas already exposes a fully documented REST API at the institution level.
📋 CLASS_ENTRY dicts ❓ Assignment questions 🔗 File URLs (all types) 💾 canvas_assignments_cache.json
2
File Download
hbsp_downloader.py
Routes each file by type: HBSP links → Playwright browser navigates through Canvas LTI handshake, intercepts the file response at the network layer, and saves bytes directly (no click-based download dialogs). Canvas-native files → injects session cookies from saved auth into a direct HTTP request. External links → plain authenticated GET with Content-Type detection. All files are renamed from magic bytes (not filename extension), so a "file.unknown" downloaded as a ZIP of XML becomes "file.xlsx" automatically.
📕 Case PDFs 📊 Excel exhibits (.xlsx) 📂 Canvas uploads (any type) 📹 Video URLs (noted, not downloaded)
3
AI Analysis
ai_case_analyzer.py
Extracts all text from case PDFs via pdfplumber, counts pages to determine output length target. Sends the full case text + assignment questions to claude-sonnet-4-6 with a structured JSON schema prompt. The model returns a structured analysis object with sections: executive summary, timeline, key actors, argument framework, risks, hidden assumptions, and per-question Q&A. Output word count scales proportionally: 4-page case → ~500 words, 22-page case → ~1,800 words, hard cap at 2,000 words.
🧠 Structured JSON analysis 📏 Length-calibrated output ❓ Per-question answers
4
Cheatsheet Builder
gen_cheatsheets_v2.py
Converts the JSON analysis into a formatted .docx using python-docx. Each section type renders differently: metadata as a styled table, executive summary as bullets, timeline as date-keyed rows, argument landscape as a 3-column comparison table, Q&A answers as labeled blocks with course-color accents. File is named with the canonical convention: {class_num} {COURSE} {Case Name} Case Analysis Cheatsheet.docx and saved directly to the course folder in OneDrive.
📄 .docx cheatsheet 📁 Saved to OneDrive course folder

Outlook Sync
outlook_sync.py
The technical design is complete: identify each HBS push event (e.g. "FIN2 C") by course code and date, then issue a Graph API PATCH to rename the subject to {num} {COURSE} {Case Name}, attach the cheatsheet and all downloaded files, and embed video links in the event body. The pipeline code is built. The API calls are tested.
🚫 Blocked — HBS IT has not granted Microsoft Graph API access to individual students. The Calendars.ReadWrite scope required to modify Outlook calendar events can only be authorized by an administrator with delegated authority over the tenant. Individual students cannot self-authorize. Without it, this step cannot run automatically.
Enterprise Opportunity
This is the gap between a student workaround and a school-wide solution. If HBS IT issued a service account with Calendars.ReadWrite Graph API authority, this pipeline could run automatically for every enrolled student — no individual setup required. Every class, every week: calendar events renamed with full case context in the body of the calendar event (situation, classroom discussions, links to pre-class polls, etc.) files attached (case, exhibits, course notes, etc), video links embedded. HBS already pushes generic calendar events to students; the infrastructure is there. One IT decision turns a personal productivity tool into an institutional one — and fixes what is, frankly, a poorly integrated platform that forces students to manually reconstruct context that the school's own systems already contain.
